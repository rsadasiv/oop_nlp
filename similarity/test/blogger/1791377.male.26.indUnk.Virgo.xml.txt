BEIJING (Reuters) - China is conducting emergency safety checks at SARS laboratories nationwide after a leak in Beijing spawned a web of infection that led to the first reported death from the virus since a major outbreak last year.  Scientists had sealed off laboratories at the National Institute of Virology in Beijing, where a student caught the deadly disease while conducting research involving the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome coronavirus, the Beijing News said.  Five other teams of experts had fanned out to provincial laboratories, it said.  Technical experts from the World Health Organisation were to arrive in China's capital, Beijing, this week to assist in the investigation and possibly to advise the government on control practices, the WHO said.  "It appears that there were lapses in bio-safety guidelines," said Beijing-based WHO spokesman Bob Dietz. "That is what they'll be investigating."  SARS spread alarm across the world last year and had a serious economic impact throughout Asia as tourism and investment were both hard hit.  Fearing a spread, China has stepped up surveillance of passengers for SARS ahead of a week-long holiday starting May 1, when millions of people are expected to travel by air or rail.  More than 180 staff of the Beijing institute, part of the national centre for disease control, had been moved to a resort north of the city and placed under medical observation, the newspaper said.  The institute and other disease control offices had taken up temporary quarters in another part of the capital after their laboratories were sealed, it said.  "WHO sees the current situation in China as one requiring heightened vigilance, but still not one of a significant threat to public health," Julie Hall, the WHO's SARS team leader in China, said in a statement.  All of the cases diagnosed in the most recent outbreak, two confirmed and six suspected, were traceable to the laboratory.  The woman who died had been taking care of her daughter, a 26-year-old student, surnamed Song, who spent two weeks in the laboratory in March.  The second confirmed victim, a 20-year-old nurse surnamed Li, took care of Song in a Beijing hospital earlier this month. Both Li and Song were in stable condition without fever, state media said.
